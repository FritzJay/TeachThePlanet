Elevation and Shadows

The place we create the heirarcy of objects and components in our applications.

Our application uses white space to create a minimalist effect so that teachers can focus quickly on the key aspects of each app. To create balance and a sense of physical space we use the following elevation and shadow effects to ensure content does not get lost in space. We base our practices on Google's Material Design handbook and our own preferences.

How It Works

Objects in apps act like objects in the physical world. Thanks to material design we follow these simple rules:

  • Objects can't pass through one another.
  • Objects can stack or be affixed to one another causing them to create shadows and reflect light.

Elevation Breakdown

Elevation

Elevation is the distance, or depth between the surface of two objects along the z-axis. Elevation is measured in density-independent pixels (dp) similarlly to the x and y axes. All objects have depth, meaning all materials are 1dp thick.

Resting Elevation

Resting elevation is the home base for an object, think default. Different types of objects or components have consistent restting elevation across our apps, though they may have different resting elevations across platforms and devices.

Responsive Elevation

Responsive elevation is the offset created when a user interacts with a object or component. It is relative to the resting state and moves from one elevation to another. A button's elevation, receiving user input will go through three different elevations as it responds (eg., normal, focused, and pressed).
